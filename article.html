The PR curve looks simple but most people don't know what to look for. Here are the three rules that tell you which model actually wins — and why the obvious choice can be wrong.

Rule 1 — area under the curve
The shaded area = Average Precision (AP). Bigger area means better model overall. Think of it as the model's total score across every possible threshold — not just one operating point.

Rule 2 — top-right corner
A perfect model stays at Precision = 1.0 all the way to Recall = 1.0. The closer a curve hugs the top-right corner, the better. Which model drops first as Recall increases?
Model A — best Stays above 0.90 precision until Recall ≈ 0.80. Even at high recall it barely drops.
Model B — moderate Drops below 0.90 around Recall ≈ 0.65. Respectable but starts fading earlier.
Model C — weakest Starts high but dives sharply — precision falls fast after Recall ≈ 0.75.
Model A hugs the top-right corner the longest — the best balance of precision and recall across all operating points.
Rule 3 — cliff drop warning
A sudden steep dive in precision means the model becomes unreliable the moment you push recall higher. Lower threshold to catch more positives → precision collapses → floods of false alarms.
Model C — has a cliff (dangerous) Precision crashes from ~0.85 to ~0.50 in a short span around Recall 0.80. The dashed line on the chart marks this cliff. Avoid for fraud detection, disease screening — any task needing high recall.
Model A — gentle slope (safe) Even at very high recall, precision drops gradually and predictably. You can push the threshold without the model suddenly flooding you with false alarms.
Model C's cliff is invisible on the ROC curve — only the PR curve reveals it. This is exactly why both charts matter.

Accuracy vs Precision vs Recall

As humans, we've always made decisions using these kinds of metrics — even if we didn't know their names or realize it. Let me help you in realizing it.

Machine Learning Algorithms can be broadly categorized into supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning. In this article we focus on classification model metrics.

The Investigator Puzzle

Police hired a private investigator to identify murderers among innocents. The investigator has 7 days. On which day did he perform best?

Use your gut — don't think about metrics yet.

* = Declared guilty by investigator M = Actual murderer I = Actual innocent
Which day was best?
The word "best" is subjective

The answer depends entirely on the context and the problem you're trying to solve. Once the problem is well-defined, the right metric becomes obvious.

⚖️
Law & Justice
"It is better that ten guilty persons escape than one innocent suffer."

Minimize false positives. When the system flags someone as guilty, it must be very likely correct.

Precision matters more
🚁
Search & Rescue

Drones scanning a forest after a plane crash. The goal is to find every single person who might be alive. Missing someone could cost a life.

False alarms are acceptable — rescuers can investigate. But missing a real survivor? Never.

Recall matters more
So what are these metrics?
Precision
TP / (TP + FP)
Of everyone the investigator declared guilty, how many were actual murderers? Measures quality of positive predictions.
Recall
TP / (TP + FN)
Of all actual murderers present, how many did the investigator catch? Measures how many real positives were found.
Accuracy
(TP + TN) / Total
How many times was the investigator simply right — on everyone, murderers and innocents alike?
These metrics really matter when most things are going right, and only a few go wrong — what machine learning calls imbalanced classification, where one outcome happens far more often than the other.
The ideal vs reality

High Precision, High Recall, and High Accuracy is ideal — but far from reality. Just like infinity, we can always get closer and closer, but never reach 100%.

It therefore becomes essential to ask your business: which metric makes you most profitable, and which can you slightly compromise on — based on your core problem.
